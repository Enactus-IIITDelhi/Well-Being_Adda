What is Well-being?

"Health is wealth," we've all heard the age-old adage. But have we truly understood what it means? Every moment of our lives affects and is affected by the state of our health and well-being: be it our performance at school, college or work, our relationships with friends and family or even the moments we spend with just ourselves. It is a reflection of how we feel and function in our everyday lives. What exactly is a state of well-being, though? Whatever we define it to be for ourselves! Each of us defines it differently for ourselves based on our unique personalities, value systems and ambitions. The traditional notion of health only advocates physical fitness but realising just how intrinsic a role well-being plays in our lives, we must revise this definition to include the diverse and interconnected dimensions of our lives. Well-being isn't merely the absence of physical ailments but a state of comfort and happiness. It encompasses the physical as well as psychological, social, spiritual and intellectual realms. Each of these five spheres of well-being is equally important and no less interdependent.

Facets of well-being

PHYSICAL WELL-BEING is most similar to the traditional notion of health and includes taking care of one’s body. It involves sleeping well, eating healthy, staying hydrated, exercising regularly and avoiding preventable diseases. Physical well-being also means a balance of mind and body, which is developed by making time for relaxation and enjoyment.

PSYCHOLOGICAL WELL-BEING refers to mental and emotional health. It refers to the combination of feeling good and functioning effectively. Emotional well-being is being able to handle both pleasant and unpleasant emotions effectively. Mental health not only refers to the absence of mental illnesses but also includes factors such as our ability to manage stress and be autonomous. Practising kindness, gratitude, and staying connected with loved ones, all contribute to psychological well-being.

SOCIAL WELL-BEING refers to our ability to make and maintain meaningful positive relationships with our family, friends, neighbours and co-workers. It involves being able to co-exist in a harmonious, stable and peaceful social setting and engage in mutual dependence. Morals, ethics, communication, kindness and gratitude are all important factors that contribute to it.

SPIRITUAL WELL-BEING is about developing one’s fundamental values and finding one’s purpose in life. It involves connecting with oneself and nature and expression through art, music, literature, etc. It is often associated with greater resilience, happiness and fulfillment in life.

INTELLECTUAL WELL-BEING refers to the will to learn, think critically and engage in creative and mentally stimulating activities. It includes expanding and sharing our knowledge and skills. Academics, personal hobbies, cultural and community involvement develop intellectual well-being.

The different dimensions of well-being are closely interlinked and form an ecosystem of our body and mind to maintain the kind of innate balance that one associates with a sense of well-being. As we strive towards holistic well-being, we must give equal importance to nurturing each of these facets.
